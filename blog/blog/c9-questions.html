Asking Good Questions

January 29, 2015

I have learned over the last couple of months that one incredibly important aspect of the programmer and developer community is asking questions. There are an unbelievable amount of resources online for answering coding questions. Though stackoverflow.com is the major one, there are ton's of technical blogs and Youtube tutorials available to help you when your stuck. What's important about using these resources is that you both understand your question, and understand how to ask it.

Coding questions can be difficult to ask. They are full of technical terms, abbreviations, implied syntax, etc. Understand how to break down a difficult problem into a specific question can be difficult, but thinking through it in this process can be extremely helpful. You may even find (as I have), that the process of asking a questions helps you to understand the problem better, and ultimately figure it out yourself.

Good questions should have the following:

  • - Write a title that summarizes the specific problem
  • - Pretend you're talking to a busy colleague, i.e keep it consice and clear
  • - Make sure spelling, grammar look good, otherwise you won't be taken seriously
  • - Introduce the problem before you post any code
  • - Include example code
  • - Help others reproduce the problem
  • - Respond to feedback

With my experience scrolling through stackoverflow.com, the best questions usually yield the shortest, most concise, and most accurate answers. When an open-ended or unclear question is asked, the answer are so all over the place, it's usually just more confusing the read it.

Code is a language, and understanding that language is vital to understand how to ask a question. The better you can "speak code," the more clear your question will be to the community of people that understand the language. This has taken me some time, though I can clearly see I'm getting better at it. A couple months ago, phrasing a question about ruby would be impossible. I simply wouldn't have the vocabulary to speak about it. But I have learned so much in the last couple of weeks that I feel much more confident now that I can have a conversation in the language of code.

Asking questions is a vital part of learning, and it's important to keep the concepts in mind as I move forward in my experience as a developer.
